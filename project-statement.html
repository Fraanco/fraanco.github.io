Investors' Statement.

The Investors' Statement allowed Liber Capital's users to easily visualize the transactions they did with the service. The user friendly interface was very well received by the users, since it provided more control regarding their investments and its returns.

Role.

Researcher, Interaction Designer

Client.

Liber Capital

Year.

2018

Context

When you are a investor at Liber Capital, you can invest your money in invoices (the accounts receivables of small and medium businesses). It is a high risk and high profitability sort of investment, and normally deals with large sums of money. Given those constraints, our financier target group usually consists of experienced investors.

As soon as investors start investing at Liber Capital, it is only fair that they can see what is going on with their money: how much do they have with us? When did they buy invoices? When were the invoices settled? When did they deposit or remove money from their "Liber Account"?

Those were the problems the Investors' Statement project aimed to solve.

Briefing

The appearance and navigation of the bank statement should be similar to the ones found in traditional bank websites (or even ATMs) and other digital brokers.

It is worth saying here that familiarity is one of the core principles of our design system - some of our users are tech savvy, but most of them are not, thus we must provide innovative experiences that are recognizable by most users.

The feature should be designed for web and for our mobile apps. This is a sort of a bad habit we have at Liber Capital: within the same project (with time constraints and everything) I need to design the web and mobile (iOS and Android version) of the same features.

Even though this may sound okay for consistency sake, the time is not enough so every form factor receive its deserved attention. A big downside is that Android and iOS don't usually have the needed focus on their systems' peculiarities.

Research.

Research

As I didn't have much time for research in this project (and since I wasn't supposed to reinvent the statement), I opted for a reference-based research at first, to then approach internal and external users once I had a prototype to gather feedback.

Once the team brought me the briefing of the project we needed to solve, I did my own internal briefing (as I usually do) and raised some points I would need to figure out during the research.

  • How should be the dates visually organized in this feature?
  • Should there be a "monthly summary" in every month?
  • Will the Web and Mobile options of the bank statement have the same features?
  • What would be the time frames we would allow the users to access?
  • Should we provide access to the invoices' purchase agreements from the bank statement?
  • Should the earnings of every investment be shown separately from the total valued?

Benchmarks

I then started the research looking for references in other institutions' statements. For that I analyzed traditional and digital banks, digital brokers and dribbble shots, aiming to figure out how detailed their pages were, how they organized the dates and what information they showed.

In most of the examples found, the statements were separated by month. Even though other time frames were allowed, the standard search was based on specific months.

The standard time frame shown is usually "Last 30 days".

Regarding the order of the entries, even though all of them presented chronological ordination (thankfully) I found two main approaches: some institutions started with the oldest transactions, while others would start with the most recent.

Regarding the informations available, I also found some different approaches. Some were straight-up very detailed: showing date, type of transaction, value moved, description, final balance - for every entry. Some would just show the most important information upfront (date, type, value a very summarized description) and give the users the option to see more by clicking on them.

Ideation.

With the first part of the research done and the references gathered, I started planning the propositions based on our needs and specificities.

One of my favorite activities doing this step of the process is listing the 1. main information the user should be able to find on the feature or page; and 2. main actions the user should be able to perform in the feature or page.

    Operation types

  • Withdrawal (negative)
  • Invoice acquisition (negative)
  • Deposit (positive)
  • Invoice Settling (positive)

    • Information

  • Date the transaction happened
  • Type of operation
  • Description (list of the invoices bought)
  • Operation details
  • Taxes

    • Actions

  • Navigate (look at different time frames)
  • Filter (operations by kind)
  • Sort
  • Access more details

First Iteration

Focused on the most experienced financiers - used to traditional and information-dense interfaces - the first idea would show the classical statement table used by traditional institutions. It would show positive and negative transactions in separate columns and present the final statement after every entry. The mobile version would be a summarized version of the same table.

In both versions (web and mobile) the user would be able to get more information regarding the invoice acquisition and settling operations, which would be shown in a modal.

The standard time frame shown is usually "Last 30 days".

Feedbacks

Using this first design I made a clickable prototype to get feedback from some users. I went around the office asking some colleagues (programmers, sales representatives, the lawyer and even the CEO) to give it a try and collected some feedbacks. I also sent the link to the prototype to some of our actual users - since they are usually busy, I couldn't get a proper test, but collected their feedback anyway.

Even though the feedback was quite positive, I felt during the conversations that I could try something different for our statement. After all, we're a fintech and we should present simpler and friendlier solutions to our costumers!

Final Version.

Talking to my manager, we decided to try something more minimalistic and modern. More than anything, we wanted to get rid of the table component. So I proposed some changes:

  • The statement is now presented as a timeline.
  • It uses icons to describe the type of the operation, and colors to indicate when the money was coming in or out their accounts.
  • The entries are aggregated by operation, and the details about the invoices related to each operation can be accessed at the same screen (no more modals!). Also, in the web version, a mouse-over gives the user informations regarding taxes.
  • The option to search for specific entries within the statement, to access its details.
  • A "Summary of the Period" component (on the web version), giving the user an fast and easy access to the total amount of money handled during the period.

    • New actions:

  • Invoice acquisition contracts' download
  • Money transfer receipt
  • Download Pdf statement
  • Download Xslx statement

Final Thoughts.

The Financier Statement was a very interesting project to work on. Even though I didn't have the opportunity to do as much research as I would ideally like to, I managed to use the time I had to experiment, collect feedback and take informed decisions.

The users' reception for this feature was amazing. Firstly because now they wouldn't need to download xlsx reports to find out about their transactions; and second because they could find all the information they needed in a simple (and user friendly) way.

Learnings

Looking for references can be an amazing way of starting the research for my projects, but I need to be careful with the creative constraints it can generate. (Don't get stuck with an idea before exploring the possibilities!)

What I should have done differently

• Explore different ideas before getting stuck with the first (and finishing the whole design before trying something else)

• Try to get some feedback with medium and low fidelity prototypes (before finishing the whole design)
