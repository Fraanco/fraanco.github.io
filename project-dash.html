Investor's Dashboard.

The Investors' dashboard was a groundbreaking project to Liber Capital, for both ⠀the team⠀ (because of the way we managed to work on it since the beginning) and ⠀the users⠀ (because it delivered value right away and changed the way they perceived our product).

In short, this feature's main objective was to give our users some transparency about their investments within our product. As the first screen, it shows the users right away ⠀the most important information⠀ (such as how much money is invested, how much they can withdraw, how much they've earned and a calendar of the future events), and gives access to ⠀the most important actions⠀ (such as withdrawing money).

Role.

UX Researcher, Interaction Designer

Client.

Liber Capital

Year.

2018

Context.

Problem Definition

The way this project came to being was very interesting though, and it's what makes me love it even more. Liber's CEO asked me to work on another feature, which would ask investors to define specifications of the investments they were interested on (based on price, profitability, risk, time, and etc). The idea was to make it easier for our operations team to offer the users the available investments.

The first step for this project was to talk to the operations team, and figure out how they usually got those informations from users. My findings on this stage were much richer, though. Talking to one of our main team members I found out why the CEO wanted this feature, and could dig deeper to find what was the real problem we needed to solve:

  • The operation team was overwhelmed with calls from users.

    Some of the calls were related to new investments (problem which would probably be addressed by the original project), but most of them were from users asking for reports about their investments.

  • The users needed to call and ask for the reports because they couldn't find the information on our application.

    They needed to call our team even if they wanted to know how much money they had with us, for example.

Thus, the real problem we needed to solve was the lack of information available to the users, and a good way to measure the success of the project was the decrease in the number of calls to the CX teams.

Research.

The team

The first step was to ⠀interview the team⠀. That helped me to better understand the investor's persona, and the main questions they had; how was the operations contact with the users (what did they do for them, how did they explain the service and the product).

For this stage I tried to talk to a multidisciplinary group, to get as many information as possible about how we dealt (and how they thing we should deal) with our users. I talked to 7 team members in total: the CEO and the CTO - to understand the business and product vision for that feature; two members of the Costumer Success team - to understand their daily life, and their feelings about the user's experience ; 2 members of the finance team - to reach a better understanding of investment jargons and other specificities of the field; and the company's lawyer.

Although the questions would vary a little depending on the interviewee's field of work and specialty, the aim of the Team Interview was to cover some basic topics:

  • How was the onboarding of new financiers done at the time?

    Who made the first contact?
    What were their main initial questions?
    Did they express their first impressions?

  • What were our team's routines like?

    How was their work like regarding this specific type of user?
    What did they usually ask?
    Was there a problem that would appear constantly?

  • What was our target audience for this type of user?

    What how were our actual users like?
    How experienced they were with finances and investment issues?
    Did they understand the risks?

The Users

Then I ⠀interviewed our users⠀. That helped me to validate some assumptions about the personas, understand their expectations and frustrations with the product and hear some suggestions and feedbacks.

This step of the process was very valuable, since it enlightened me about other products our users were using (benchmarking!) and their level of experience with our product's market and context (financial investments and supply chain financing).

Most users complained about the lack of information within the product (which validated the problem statement) and shared with me the spreadsheets they were creating to keep track of what was happening with their money (first hand inspiration!).

Benchmarks

Next step was ⠀benchmarking⠀for the feature, checking how similar products solved the same problem. This phase of researched aimed to find inspiration both for the usability (information architecture and industry standards) and visual aspects of our dashboard.

Ideation.

With the material from the research gathered, I started sketching a proposition for the solution. This was a iterative process involving the operations team, the CEO and a few users.

First, I listed the most relevant ⠀information⠀information and the ⠀main actions⠀ to be shown in the dashboard:

    Information

  • Total patrimony (all the money the user have sent us)
  • Invested money (the share of this money currently invested in invoices)
  • Available balance (the share of the money that could be withdrawn or invested)
  • Investment distribution (the percentage of money invested in each of our partners)
  • Average profitability
  • Weighted average term
  • Date and value of the next settlement
  • Settlement schedule (showing all future and past settlements, with date and value for each one)
  • Accumulated income (this was sort of a vanity metric we decided to include, showing our users how much they earned by investing with Liber)

    • Actions

  • Opening a list of current invoices
  • Search options within the settlement schedule.

UX/UI Design.

There was another constraint in this project: the CEO wanted us to release it fast. So, in a meeting with the Development team leader at the time we decided to experiment a different process. It is worth highlighting that it was an experiment, and that our aim was to provide value as quick as possible (but also in the best way possible).

The interface would be created in ⠀modules⠀, and as soon the wireframe of each module was completed and approved, they were handed to the backend developers so they could create the API.

The UI Design for each module would need to be done "simultaneously" for Web and for the App. The mobile developer and the frontend developer would then work at each module and incrementally release the feature.

Final Thoughts.

Learnings

It is incredibly tempting for stakeholders to reach us with an idea of a solution (a new feature or an extra screen on the website, for example), but it is our duty as designers to ⠀understand what the problem is⠀ before diving into wireframing and prototyping. We need to start from the problem we need to solve, so we can check if the solution we came up with is solving it.

What I should have done differently

• More time to create specific designs for each form factor. The design for this feature was made with the web form factor in mind (since the most used devices to access our product were notebooks and desktops at the time). Some of the modules should have a better presentation in the mobile app, since the adaptation didn't work too well for it.t

• I wish I had more time to test the modules before developing. In a future project, the combination of the modular designing and developing could count with some testing before starting the development.
