Vai Pa Onde?

Vai Pa Onde was a collaborative website that gathers the free online activities people can do without leaving the comfort of your home: Courses, exercises, recipes, ebooks - everything for free! It was created to organize and help people find stuff to do at home without much digging.

Context

Some people saw the 2020's quarantine as an opportunity to stay home and improve themselves: taking multiple courses, reading articles, enjoying live events, listening to podcasts and etc... Others, on the other hand, couldn't handle the conditions of the "new normal".

On both niches, companies saw great marketing opportunities: some provided free courses, some provided a few extra free months of their service, and some just created content marketing articles on "how to prepare your home office environment", "how to keep your exercise routine from home", and similar subjects.

The amount of free content available was very exciting, but it was quite hard to find them when you wanted.

Vai Pa Onde was a collaborative website that gathers the free online activities people can do without leaving the comfort of your home: Courses, exercises, recipes, ebooks - everything for free! It was created to organize and help people find stuff to do at home without much digging.

Problem Definition

Vai Pa Onde is a personal project, conceived and developed during (and because of) the Quarantine. As such, it gave me the opportunity to experiment a lot, both with the product's functionalities and overall visual aspect.

Given the context described in the Introduction, the idea of which problem to address came from something that happened to me: I'm an online course freak, and I was absolutely stunned by the courses that became free during the quarantine. However, I was coming across many of them on social media, email, or even WhatsApp messages, and when I had the actual free time to take some classes I couldn't remember where to find them - and would spend most of that spare time trying to find it them.

Thus I chose to address the amount of free content made available, and how hard it was to find it.

Research.

Research

Were people experiencing the same thing I was? How were they coping with the amount of free content becoming available everywhere? Did people even know how much free knowledge was available to them?

Actually... how much free knowledge was available for us out there?

At this point, I used the research stage to explore two main topics:

  • The Available Content

    What kind of content was out there? Was everything actually for free? What were the covered topics? Was everything related to covid19?

  • Content Consumption

    Were people actually looking for any content? And if so, where were they looking? Were they finding anything? What were they doing with their quarantine time?

The Available Content

For the first topic, It was mostly a treasure hunt:

I went back to my email inbox and checked out everything I received from companies - newsletters, heart-warming messages, extended trials and etc.

Social media was a great source as well: companies sharing everything they were giving out for free, Instagrams profiles started to create specific content for quarantine times (such as chefs teaching easy recipes or personal trainers suggesting exercises to do at home).

Finally I went to google to see what else I could find. There was a lot out there. I even found a collaborative google sheet for free content (so the problem was real).



Content Consumption

Quick Interviews to understand how others were spending their spare time during the quarantine. If they were actually taking courses and watching lives, how were they finding them?

Hashtags and Trending Topics

Google Trends: how did the google search for some topics (such as "free courses" or "home office") change with the beginning of the quarantine?

Ideation

Once I saw some people were having similar issues with the amount of information, it was time to decide what kind of solution could be built. At that point, I started looking for people to help me bring the idea to life.

For the format, we decided to create a website. We thought about creating an app, but it would be harder to develop, restricted to mobile devices, and harder to convince people to use it. We thought about creating a Newsletter, but... isn't this exactly the problem we're trying to solve?

We even thought about a simple shared google spreadsheet just to validate the idea, but we've found one during research, so that ship was sailed.

For the content, we decided to start with quarantine related only. But shortly we started to share everything that was free and possible to be done at home. This decision was made so we have more diversity of content - and so the project wouldn't necessarily die off once the quarantine was over.

Interaction Design.

Information Architecture

There were two crucial factors that drove most of the website UX decisions: Organization & Collaboration

Organization:

The main point was to help people find what to do for free and without leaving their homes. Vai Pa Onde should be like a tourist information center: you get there with a vague objective, it show you what's available, why it can be good and how to get there.

With that in mind, we tried to organize everything we found on four main categories:

  • Aprender ("to learn")

    online courses, specializations, congresses, mentorships and etc.

  • Participar ("to participate")

    Time-bond and live events

  • Fazer ("to do")

    Suggestions of activities, new free games or apps

  • Compartilhar ("to share")

    Cool instagram profiles, youtube channels, articles, information regarding covid19, tips on mental health.

On top of that, we created some sub-categories and tags, to help users navigate and the website to make suggestions.

Collaboration:

  • We're exploring the internet with our users. If it is on the website it is easier for other people to find. We needed to make it easy for people to share what they found.

  • Quality Assurance: The plan was to have as many entries as possible, but at the same time we were only three people working on the project. We needed people to help us validate if the links were working if an activity expired, for instance

User Interface

Content Centred

• As the website is focused on gathering content from other platforms, its visual aspects were designed focusing on the original content information (name of the course, responsible entity); and on the organization and sorting according to the website's categories, improving navigation.



Bold and playful

• The typography and colors were selected for two main reasons: readability and impact. We needed the titles of the entries to be easy to read and, well, to look good as a standalone text.

Release and Iterations.

MVP

We released a minimal viable product to collect user insights and feedback. We focused on a small group and followed their considerations closely. Then we extracted some insights:

The main point was to help people find what to do for free and without leaving their homes. Vai Pa Onde should be like a tourist information center: you get there with a vague objective, it show you what's available, why it can be good and how to get there.

  • It is still a mess

    The landing page showed a list of all the entries, without explanations of what the colors meant. We were gathering content, but we didn't make it easier to consume.

  • Some people won't even know what it is about

    The first version didn't have a tagline or explanation about the project, so new users wouldn't easily understand what the entries were about (not before going through the "about" page, at least).

  • Contribute? with money? no thank you.

    We noticed no one clicked the button to suggest entries. At the same time, people were sending us links via WhatsApp - so they did have suggestions. User interviews showed us that people thought that the "Contribute" button was to contribute with money. Huh.

Final Version

Based on the feedbacks on the MVP, we made some changes and released a "Final Version". Of course, following user activity using Hotjar allowed us to find new problems to fix - and we released 6 new versions with fixes and new features:

  • Tagline explaining the website

    We added the tagline to the website's header: "The portal with everything so you can learn without leaving home".

  • UX writing improvements

    Button: Contribute → Submit a suggestion (only then we started getting suggestions through the form).

  • Home Screen

    We added a home screen explaining each category available, the ammount of entries each one had and how many entries were added/expiring that day.

  • Favorites

    As the list of contents grew bigger, we added the possibility for users to save entries as favorites. That would allow users to filter entries they wanted to check out later.

  • Personalized Lists

    We then saw the opportunity of creating a feature that would: 1. Allow people to curate the content website according to their own needs, creating multiple personalised lists; 2. Encourage people to share their lists (and, thus, sharing our website).

  • Courses only

    After many months of pandemics and quarantine, the corporate production of free content and courses didn't keep up. In addition, people became saturated with lives and other online events.

    Thus, we opted to pivot the purpose of the site, reorganizing the categories focusing mainly on free courses and educational content.

    The new categories created were: Courses with Certificate; Courses without Certificate; Events (For congresses, conferences and other limited-time actions) Content (youtube, instagram or tik tok profiles, but always focusing on educational content).
