Impeach TV.

As a result of my undergraduate research I created a prototype for an interactive audiovisual tool. It used video metadata, algorithmic editing, and user interaction to provide the emergence of new meanings from an audiovisual content.

Role.

Researcher, Interaction Designer and Developer

Client.

State University of Campinas (UNICAMP)

Year.

2016

Context.

In April 2016, I began my research on “The use of metadata on the development of interactive audiovisual narratives,” financed by the São Paulo Research Foundation. Employing previous experiences and knowledge of database narratives and algorithmic editing, I developed a prototype that edited videos in real-time based on a database of video fragments and user interaction.

Ideation

I designed and tested the tool with real users in educational, artistic, and political contexts, the latter being the most successful. Using fragments of the audiovisual record of congress voting for Dilma Rousseff’s impeachment, the prototype invited users to watch the nine hours of footage edited in real-time based on their choices regarding the voters’ characteristics (e.g., party, gender, position on impeachment)

Testing.

To understand the result of the prototype, and how the interaction impacted user consumption and understanding of the content I tested it with users in two contexts:
1. An in depth test interview with five users after ten minutes using the prototype;
2. A survey with ten questions, taken during Unicamp's Undergraduate research congress.

Interviews

The interviews were set in a controlled environment, in which users had access to a computer and headphones to interact with the prototype. Interviwees did not have any knowledge about the research and the prototype beforehand. Following the ten minutes use of the prototype, users went through a recorded interview.

The purpose of the interview was to understand:
1. if the user understood what the prototype was about (the audiovisual content and the user interface);
2. If they understood how to interact with the user interface;
3. If they actually interacted with the content;
4. And what was their overall interpretation of the content.

Survey

With the questions raised during the bibliographic research and during user interviews, I developed a 10 questions survey to be taken by users during Unicamp's undergraduate research congress. During the congress, users were invited to interact with the prototype and answer the survey right after.

The survey was divided in 3 parts:
1. The User Interface
In which the objective was to find out if the prototype worked properly and if the UI was intuitive;
2. The User Experience
In which users were asked if the users understood how to interact with the prototype and if they felt their interaction was meaningful;
3. Content Interpretation
In which users were asked about the actual content streamed with the prototype, what did they think it was, and if they felt their interaction affected their interpretation.

Results.

Interviews of testers revealed that having ownership over how to consume the content provoked different interpretations on the subject. This highlighted the importance of interaction design over information consumption in interactive media.

Learnings

In a practical sense, the prototype demonstrated the tool’s unviability without automated video description and categorization (manual creation being too onerous).

From an academic perspective, however, it allowed me to present and publish two articles: “Remix and digital curatorship: The use of filters as discursive tools,” which discussed the relevance of filters for news consumption, and “Emotional design and meaning creation: The role of user experience in new media creative processes” (presented at South America’s biggest User Interaction Design Congress and published in the Brazilian academic journal, Human Factor in Design).
