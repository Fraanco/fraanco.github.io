Planalto Fighter.

Planalto Fighters was a game about the Transmission of political ideas. In fact, it was a game about the transmission of fake news by voters - and even high-ranked politicians - that, aiming to support their candidates (or to undermine their opposition), share "information" on social media without reassuring its truth.

Role.

Creative Director, Game Designer, Visual Designer

Client.

Personal Project

Year.

2018

Context.

Considering the heated political situation in Brazil in which the polarization between left-wing and right-wing candidates led to the weaponization of fake news and misinformation became a big challenge to the country's young democracy.

The game was created during the 2018 edition of the Global Game Jam (in which the theme was "Transmission") and was developed during one weekend by two people.

The Game

Planalto fighter invites the players to pick a side in this rally fight. Collaborating with other players, they must "attack their enemy" by answering simple true or false questions based on fake and true information about the candidates during the elections.

Gameplay.

Picking your side

Planalto Fighters was an online web-based social game. The first screen asked the user to pick their sides between the two most relevant political figures at the time: the Labour Party Lula and the ultraright candidate Jair Bolsonaro.

Joining the Field

Once the player picked their side, they joined the playing field, being able to see the other players online. Each player was represented by a new character wearing a shirt with the color of the side they picked (red for left-wing, blue for right-wing)

Score

The core gameplay was quite simple: players would be presented with claims regarding both candidates based on the content being spread both on social media and official media outlets and asked to choose if it was true or false.

Every time a player get an answer right, they score a point. Points are counted both individually and collectively (for each of the teams). When they get the answer wrong, the right answer is presented with the information source, and they lose a point from their scores (individual and collective).

Some examples of statements players needed to find out if were true or false:

- In 2003 Lula was considered to receive the Nobel Peace Prize. [True] - Source

- In a 1999 interview, Bolsonaro said that former Venezuelan President Hugo Chávez is “a hope for Latin America”. [True] - Source

- In a recent edition, the British magazine The Economist published a list of the 10 most corrupt presidents and former presidents in history, in which Lula appeared in the seventh position. [False] - Source

- Bolsonaro promised that if elected, he would end community daycares because, according to him, "the State has no obligation to "look after the children of others" and that would be the obligation of parents”. [False] - Source

Results.

Users could consult both their own scores and the overall team score (the % of correct answers for each of the teams), the total number of answers, and the number of clicks on the sources. The game’s results could also bring interesting insights as a thermometer for fake news recognition among the players on each extreme of the Brazilian current political spectrum.
